# Frame Rate


---

Remember those cool little flipbooks where each page had a slightly different image? When you flipped through them quickly, the image seemed to move!



![ ](//outscal.github.io/Full-Stack-Game-Development/images/0d410d3d82fda165.gif)

**Flipbook**



That's basically how videos work. Whether it's digital or film, a video is just a bunch of still images played in order. 
Each of these images? We call it a "frame".

Now, imagine flipping through that flipbook. 

How fast do you flip? 
That's kind of what frame rate is all about. 

Frame rate is just how quickly those frames change. It's measured in **Frames Per Second** or **FPS**.



> **📖 🎮 GAME DEV DICTIONARY** 
> ***Frame Rate:*** "The frequency at which consecutive images (frames) appear on a display. Measured in frames per second (FPS), it determines how smooth motion appears in games and animations."



Ever heard gamers talk about FPS? That's what they mean - how many frames their game shows each second. (not first-person shooter🫣)

Think about it this way: 

If you flip through your flipbook really slowly, the movement looks jerky, right? 
But if you flip quickly, it looks smooth!



# Frame Rate in Games


---

It's the same with videos and games:

- More FPS = smoother motion
- Less FPS = choppier motion

Most movies run at 24 FPS. That's enough to trick our brains into seeing smooth motion. But games? They often aim higher:

- 30 FPS is pretty standard
- 60 FPS is considered smooth
- Some games even go for 120 FPS or more!

![FPS Comparison](//outscal.github.io/Full-Stack-Game-Development/images/58175384558b2e8a.gif)

**60 FPS vs 30 FPS vs 24 FPS**



Why the difference? Well, in games, you're not just watching - you're interacting. 
The more frames, the more responsive it feels.

But here's the thing: not all devices can handle high frame rates.

- A powerful gaming PC might easily hit 120 FPS
- An older smartphone might struggle to reach even 30 FPS.



And even on the same device, the frame rate can change:

- In a simple scene, you might get high FPS
- In a complex scene with lots happening, FPS might drop

So, when you're making games, you've got to think about frame rates.

Every game developer faces this challenge.



How can you ensure that your game looks and plays great, no matter the FPS?🤔
