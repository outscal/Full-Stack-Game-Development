Imagine you're playing a multiplayer game such as **Call of Duty**. In this game, you control a soldier, have frequent encounters with enemies, make split-second decisions, and try not to get killed.



But you can't play this action-packed game alone. You need friends and foes to join since it's a multiplayer game.

Luckily, the game connects you with other players online, helping you find your opponents and teammates to play with.

This process is called **Matchmaking**.



## matchmaking


---

 Have you ever wondered how matchmaking actually works?

Click here to see the answer!

In the matchmaking process, the system considers your preferences and playing style to set search parameters ( e.g., your rank. ).

Then, it carefully scans through other online players, filtering out those who might not match your criteria.



Finally, it picks out matches that it thinks you'll find challenging and enjoyable to play against.

Let's take an example of another game, Chess. 

Each piece in Chess has a unique move set. 



For example, let's look at the Knight moves.



![Valid moves for Knight](https://outscal-assets.s3.ap-south-1.amazonaws.com/production/LMS/664c357050602831d2c379d3/05_24_2024__07_03_00.png)

How does the game highlight the valid moves?

Click here to see the answer!Of all the 64 boxes you see on the chess board (8*8), only a few valid positions are highlighted when you select the KNIGHT piece.



This happens because the game uses a search algorithm to find valid positions that your KNIGHT can move to.





The same strategy is also followed for the rest of the Chess pieces.



Both the examples mentioned above are different in nature.

This is because matchmaking in video games has a large data set ( i.e., all online players), and its size is not fixed. 

On the other hand, in Chess, the data set is small, and the size is fixed ( i.e., 8*8 ) .

## pathfinding


---

**Pathfinding** is one of the most common uses of search algorithms in games. 

Search algorithms help characters find their way around the game world, be it chasing enemies or navigating through a maze.



Let's think of a game that you must be familiar with, like **Pacman***.* 



![pathfinding in pacman](https://outscal-assets.s3.ap-south-1.amazonaws.com/production/LMS/664c357050602831d2c379d3/05_24_2024__08_54_02.gif)





The game actually uses a slightly different search algorithm for each ghost to make the game more exciting. 



Search algorithms are almost *everywhere* in game development, these are just a few examples!
